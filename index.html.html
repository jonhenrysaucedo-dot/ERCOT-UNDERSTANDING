Your Field Guide
to Texas Power

This isn't a reading assignment. It's a structured guide built to make the ERCOT market click — mentally. Start with the big picture, then go deep on what matters for your role.

The single best mental model: ERCOT is like a stock exchange, but for electricity. Generators are sellers, utilities and retailers are buyers, and ERCOT is both the exchange operator and the grid manager. Unlike stocks, the product is physical — electrons — so real-time balance is mandatory. You can't run a "short" inventory of electrons for more than a fraction of a second.

Section 01

What Is ERCOT?

Texas has its own power grid, mostly isolated from the rest of the US. ERCOT runs it. Understanding why Texas went it alone explains a lot about how the market works.

The Island Grid: Imagine the US power grid as a giant highway system where states can share traffic (electricity). Texas said "we'll build our own roads" — and they did, keeping almost all power generation and consumption inside Texas. This means no federal highway rules (FERC) apply to Texas's roads. The state sets its own speed limits. This is unusual, consequential, and very Texan.

⚡
Scale

26 Million Customers

About 90% of Texas's electric load. Peak demand hit 85,435 MW in August 2023 — roughly the output of 85 large nuclear plants all running at once.

🗺️
Coverage

52,000+ Miles of Lines

1,100+ generation units across the state. From Amarillo to Brownsville, the system operates as one synchronous AC grid.

🏛️
Structure

Nonprofit ISO

ERCOT is an Independent System Operator — it doesn't own generators or power lines. It's the neutral umpire. A 501(c)(4) nonprofit overseen by the PUCT.

🏝️
Isolation

The Texas Interconnection

Only minor DC ties to neighbors. No significant interstate electricity commerce = no FERC jurisdiction over market design. Texas regulates itself.

ERCOT's Three Core Jobs

Job 1

Keep the lights on

Balance generation and load every 5 seconds, 24/7. One imbalance and frequency deviates. Enough deviation and things break.

Job 2

Run fair markets

Administer the day-ahead and real-time energy markets so prices are set by competition, not monopoly control.

Job 3

Open access

Any generator or retailer that qualifies can connect and participate. ERCOT can't favor one company over another.

ERCOT isn't subject to FERC market oversight — but NERC reliability standards still apply. Texas RE (Texas Reliability Entity) audits ERCOT for things like frequency control, cybersecurity, and emergency planning. The market is state-regulated; the reliability standards are federal.

Section 01B

Who's In Charge?

Four groups shape how ERCOT operates. Know who they are and what they can actually do.

Primary Regulator

PUCT

Public Utility Commission of Texas. Approves all ERCOT market rule changes. Can direct ERCOT to implement reforms. Has final say on the design of Texas's electricity market. Post-2021, the PUCT Chair sits on ERCOT's board.

Neutral Watchdog

IMM (Potomac Economics)

Independent Market Monitor. Watches for price manipulation, anti-competitive behavior, and market power abuses. Publishes an annual State of the Market report. Its recommendations often become rule changes.

Reliability Standards

NERC / Texas RE

North American reliability standards still apply in ERCOT. Texas RE audits compliance. Violations can mean fines up to $1 million/day. These cover frequency, voltage, cybersecurity, and emergency planning.

Stakeholder Input

TAC & Working Groups

Technical Advisory Committee and subcommittees where generators, retailers, and consumers propose rule changes (NPRRs). Rules that stick here go to the Board, then PUCT. This is how the market adapts over time.

Board of Directors

Post-2021 reforms overhauled the board. It's now 12 members — 8 fully independent directors (no financial stake in ERCOT markets, all Texas residents) appointed by a state selection committee. Previously, industry players had board representation. That changed after Uri.

The stakeholder process matters in practice. If you're ever wondering why a market rule is the way it is, there's almost always a TAC filing or NPRR that explains the history. Rules don't just appear — they're proposed, debated, revised, and voted on in a process that can take 6–18 months.
Section 02

Market Structure

ERCOT runs what's called an "energy-only" market. No guaranteed payments just for existing — you get paid for actually producing energy or holding reserves that get used.

Energy-only vs capacity markets: Most US electricity markets (PJM, MISO) pay generators a "capacity payment" just for being available — like a retainer fee for a lawyer. ERCOT doesn't do that. Generators only earn money when they produce energy or win ancillary service contracts. The theory: if scarcity is real, prices spike high enough to make peakers profitable. The risk: if scarcity is extreme, you get Winter Storm Uri.

A forward auction for the next day's 24 hours. Co-optimizes energy + ancillary services. Financially binding — generators and loads are committed to their positions. Covers roughly 80–90% of total energy.

Settlement 2

Section 03

Day-Ahead Market

The DAM is how ERCOT secures most of its energy a day in advance. It reduces real-time volatility by pre-committing supply and giving participants price certainty.

Hotel rooms vs walk-ins: The DAM is like booking hotel rooms in advance at a guaranteed rate. Both the hotel (generator) and guest (load) know the price. If you didn't pre-book (didn't participate in DAM), you pay whatever the walk-in rate is the next morning — that's the real-time price, which could be much higher or occasionally lower. Most participants book in advance because certainty is worth something.

The DAM Timeline

10A

Offer/Bid Submission Deadline

Generators submit price-quantity offers for each of the next 24 hours at their specific node. Load entities submit demand bids. Ancillary service offers come in too.

~1P

DAM Co-Optimization Runs

ERCOT's algorithm co-optimizes energy AND ancillary services simultaneously — it finds the least-cost dispatch that satisfies energy demand AND reserve requirements. Transmission constraints are enforced here.

~2P

Results Posted

Clearing prices published for each hub, zone, and generator node. Ancillary service awards announced. Positions are now financially binding.

EVE

Reliability Unit Commitment (RUC)

If ERCOT sees a shortfall between committed supply and forecast demand, it commits additional generators out-of-market. These generators get a cost guarantee (make-whole payment). Cost socializes as uplift.

OD

Operating Day Begins

Generators follow their DA commitments. Any deviations settle at real-time LMP. Renewable output variability and forecast errors are handled in the 5-minute real-time market.

Co-Optimization — The Key Concept

Most markets clear energy and reserves separately. ERCOT's DAM clears them together in one solve. This means if it's cheaper to procure an extra 100 MW of Non-Spin by slightly increasing a gas plant's commitment, the algorithm finds that tradeoff automatically.

Co-optimization

Jointly minimizing total system cost by simultaneously choosing which generators produce energy AND which hold reserves. A generator's capacity is fungible — it can either sell energy or stand by as a reserve, and co-optimization picks the higher-value use for each MW.

ERCOT co-optimizes in the DAM but NOT in real-time (yet). Real-time runs energy dispatch with AS awards fixed from day-ahead. The IMM has been pushing for Real-Time Co-optimization (RTC) — it was rejected in 2019 as too expensive, but with more batteries and renewables, it's being reconsidered.
Section 04

Real-Time Market

Every 5 minutes, ERCOT runs a massive optimization algorithm to dispatch every generator in Texas. This is where the grid actually gets balanced, second to second.

SCED as a giant thermostat: Imagine a thermostat that controls 1,100 generators simultaneously, running every 5 minutes. Instead of temperature, it's balancing generation and demand. Instead of on/off, it's ramping each generator up or down based on their offered price. The "cheapest" thermostat adjustments happen first. That's Security-Constrained Economic Dispatch — SCED.

How SCED Works

Input 1

Offer Curves

Every generator submits a price-quantity curve: "I'll produce 100 MW at $20, another 50 MW at $35, another 50 MW at $60." SCED uses these to find cheapest dispatch.

Input 2

System Demand

Real-time load forecast for the next interval. This changes constantly — AC units cycle on/off, factories start/stop, weather shifts.

Input 3

Transmission Limits

Which lines are loaded? Which are near their limits? SCED won't dispatch in a way that would overload a transmission line, even if that's the cheapest option.

Settlement Intervals

SCED runs every 5 minutes, but settlement uses 15-minute intervals (three 5-minute periods averaged). Prices at each node are called LMPs — Locational Marginal Prices. More on those in the Nodal Pricing section.

Security-Constrained Economic Dispatch (SCED)

The real-time algorithm that dispatches generators at least cost while ensuring no transmission line exceeds its rated capacity. "Security-constrained" means it never dispatches in a way that would cause a reliability violation if one more line tripped.

The price cap for energy offers is $5,000/MWh (lowered from $9,000 after 2021). During normal conditions, prices are in the $20–$80 range. During scarcity, the ORDC mechanism adds a "shortage adder" on top of the offer price — this can push effective prices to the $5,000 cap. See the Scarcity section for details.

Section 05

Ancillary Services

The grid has to be stable at all times. Even if energy is balanced on average, sudden disruptions — a big plant trips offline — require instant backup. That's what ancillary services are for.

The hospital analogy: Energy dispatch is the scheduled care. Ancillary services are the emergency room — they exist for when something unexpected goes wrong. Just as a hospital keeps ER beds and staff on standby (not serving normal patients), ERCOT holds reserve capacity on standby. The tiers below are how fast the ER can respond: some in milliseconds, some in 30 minutes.

Fast Frequency Response (FFR) — A Deeper Look

FFR is a subset of RRS that triggers automatically within cycles (fractions of a second) when frequency drops below ~59.7 Hz. Large industrial loads on under-frequency relays (UFRs) simply disconnect instantly — reducing demand before humans can react. This is physics-based protection, not ERCOT dispatch. Batteries can also provide FFR by injecting power almost instantly.

ECRS introduced in 2023 had an unintended side effect: by holding many 10-minute-capable units in reserve, it pulled them out of the energy market. This caused some real-time energy scarcity (and higher prices) even when no emergency had occurred. The IMM flagged this. Expect ongoing calibration of how much ECRS to procure.

Post-2021 Reserve Scaling

ERCOT roughly doubled its operating reserves after Uri — from about 4 GW total (pre-2021) to around 8 GW by 2023. This conservative approach means they commit more capacity out-of-market and hold more reserves than strict economics would suggest. It's more expensive, but the reliability improvement is the point.

Section 06

Nodal Pricing (LMP)

Electricity prices aren't the same everywhere in Texas. A wind farm in Midland and a data center in Houston face completely different prices — even at the same instant. Here's why.

Traffic and toll roads: Think of power flowing like cars on a highway. If the highway between Midland (cheap wind) and Houston (big load) is congested, cheap power can't get through efficiently. The "price" of power in Houston goes up because you have to use the expensive local generator instead. The difference in price is the congestion cost — like a toll for using a bottlenecked road. CRRs (in the next section) are like toll exemption passes.

LMP Has Three Components

Component 1

Energy Component

The system-wide marginal cost of serving one more MWh — what the cheapest "next" generator is offering. Same across all nodes if there were zero transmission limits.

Component 2

Congestion Component

The price adjustment for transmission constraints. Positive in import-constrained (expensive) areas, negative in export-constrained (cheap, bottled-up) areas. This is where most price variation comes from.

Component 3

Loss Component

Adjustment for resistive losses on the wires. Generators far from load have a slight price discount (their power wastes more in transit). Usually minor vs congestion.

Zones and Hubs — Simplification for Trading

With 4,000+ individual nodes, ERCOT aggregates prices into:

Settlement

4 Load Zones

North, South, West, Houston. Retail customers and load-serving entities settle at their zone price — a weighted average of nodal LMPs in that region. Simpler than tracking every node.

Trading

Trading Hubs

North Hub, South Hub, West Hub, Houston Hub. Used for bilateral contracts and forward hedges. Hub prices are more stable and liquid than individual node prices — better for long-term deals.

Basis Risk

The risk that your specific node price diverges significantly from the hub price. A West Texas wind farm might be priced at $-10 at its node during a solar surge while West Hub is at $15 — a $25 basis. Managing this with CRRs is essential for project economics.

Section 07

Congestion Hedging (CRRs)

If you're a generator in West Texas selling power to Houston, you could get crushed by congestion costs. Congestion Revenue Rights exist to let you hedge that exposure financially.

Weather insurance: You're a wind developer in West Texas. You sell power via a contract at Houston Hub price. But when it's windy, the line to Houston fills up, your node price drops to $5, and Houston Hub is at $40. You lose $35/MWh to congestion. A CRR from your node to Houston Hub is like insurance — if that congestion occurs, the CRR pays you the difference. You've locked in the hub price regardless of grid conditions.

What a CRR Is

Financial Congestion Hedge

A CRR from Point A → Point B for 1 MW pays you the DAM congestion price difference (B minus A) for each hour. If A is higher, you owe the difference. It's purely financial — doesn't give you any physical scheduling rights.

Who Uses Them

Generators, Loads & Traders

Generators hedge basis risk between their node and the hub. Retailers hedge the cost of delivering power from a hub to their load zone. Traders speculate on expected congestion patterns.

How They're Acquired

Auctions & Allocations

Annual auctions (6 rounds, declining capacity: 70%→10%) plus monthly auctions. Legacy transmission customers get some pre-assigned CRRs from before deregulation.

Settlement

Paid from DAM Congestion Rent

CRR payouts come from the congestion "rent" collected in the DAM — loads in congested zones pay more, generators in bottlenecked areas earn less. That surplus funds the CRR holders.

CRRs only hedge DAM congestion — not real-time congestion. If you're settling in real-time without a day-ahead position, your CRR doesn't directly help. This creates an incentive for participants to participate in the DAM and lock in their positions — which is part of why the DAM covers 80–90% of energy.

The Declining Tranche Auction Structure

ERCOT's annual CRR auctions run in multiple rounds with decreasing amounts of capacity offered per round (starting at 70%, dropping to 10%). This prevents a single participant from locking up all the congestion hedges on a popular path. It distributes CRRs more broadly and ensures the auction price reflects genuine market consensus across multiple rounds.

Section 08

Scarcity Pricing & ORDC

ERCOT's energy-only model relies on price spikes during scarcity to compensate generators and attract new investment. The ORDC mechanism is how that scarcity gets priced.

Surge pricing: Uber surge pricing activates when there aren't enough drivers. The higher price attracts more drivers and deters some riders. ERCOT's ORDC does the same — when reserves are low, an adder is added to energy prices, rewarding units that are available. This "surge" revenue is supposed to be what makes building a peaker plant worth it in an energy-only market.

Operating Reserve Demand Curve (ORDC)

A real-time "shortage adder" added to energy prices based on how low operating reserves are. When reserves are comfortable, adder = $0. As reserves fall below thresholds, the adder climbs — up to $5,000/MWh at the worst shortage. Added to every MWh priced in that interval.

Post-2021 ORDC Changes

Change 1

Price Cap: $9,000 → $5,000/MWh

Reduced maximum exposure from extreme events. Limits the catastrophic billing that hit participants in Uri ($9,000 for 4+ days = massive defaults).

Change 2

MCL Raised to 3,000 MW

Scarcity pricing now kicks in at lower reserve levels (when more reserves remain) — more conservative trigger, more revenue to generators when moderately tight.

Change 3 (Nov 2023)

ORDC Floor Adders

$10/MWh adder when reserves below 7,000 MW; $20/MWh below 6,500 MW. Even during "normal" tight conditions, generators now earn more. A bridge measure until the PCM is implemented.

Coming Soon

Performance Credit Mechanism (PCM)

Generators earn credits for availability during top scarcity hours. LSEs must buy credits proportional to their load. A Texas-flavored capacity market — rewards performance, not just capacity. Still being finalized as of 2025.

The 2022 ORDC changes generated ~$1.7 billion in additional revenue to generators that year. That's the intended effect — make it worth building and running peakers. The debate is whether that revenue is being translated into actual new dispatchable capacity, or just enriching existing generators.

Section 09

Load Forecasting

ERCOT can't store electricity, so it has to know what's coming. Forecasting is the foundation for every commitment and dispatch decision.

Weather forecasting for electricity: ERCOT's load forecasts are fundamentally weather forecasts in disguise. Summer AC load is almost perfectly correlated with temperature. A 5°F surprise above forecast on an August afternoon can add 2–4 GW of demand in hours. Getting this wrong means either wasteful over-commitment or dangerous under-commitment.

Long-Term

Years Ahead

Capacity planning and reserve margin calculations. Published in the biannual CDR (Capacity, Demand, and Reserves) report. Incorporates expected large load additions (data centers, crypto, LNG).

Seasonal

Months Ahead

ERCOT's seasonal assessment — "do we have enough capacity for summer?" Informs whether to accelerate maintenance outage approvals or issue conservation warnings preemptively.

Day-Ahead

18–36 Hours

Used for DAM clearing and RUC decisions. Combines weather models with historical load patterns. Renewable forecasts (wind, solar) are produced separately and overlaid.

Real-Time

5-Min to 4 Hours

Short-Term Load Forecast (STLF) updated continuously. Feeds directly into SCED. Possibly ML-enhanced using real-time data signals on how fast load is ramping vs historical norms.

The December 2023 CDR introduced a new methodology that finally included expected large industrial load growth (data centers, crypto, LNG) in the forecast. The result was shocking: under those assumptions, ERCOT's reserve margin by 2029 could go negative (-2.3%) — meaning demand exceeds total expected supply. This is driving urgency around the PCM and Texas Energy Fund.

Renewable Forecasting

Wind and solar forecasts are separate from load forecasts and just as critical. All large wind and solar resources must provide real-time telemetry and weather station data to ERCOT. Specialized vendors run meteorological models for each wind "hub" region. A sudden storm front dropping wind output by 3 GW in 20 minutes is a real event that operators must anticipate. Unexpected large drops trigger ECRS and emergency reserves.

Section 10

Resource Adequacy

Do we have enough capacity to meet peak demand? Not just today — but three years from now? This is the central anxiety of grid planning in Texas.

Hotel occupancy: You need enough hotel rooms to handle peak tourist season — not just the average day. "Reserve margin" is like having 15% extra rooms available beyond expected peak demand. The problem: ERCOT doesn't have a rule that forces anyone to build those extra rooms. It relies on prices being high enough during scarcity to justify building a new hotel. Whether that works in practice is the central debate in Texas electricity policy.

Reserve Margin — The Key Metric

Target (historical)

13.75% Reserve Margin

Historically the threshold associated with roughly 1-in-10-year shortfall risk. Example: if peak demand = 80 GW, you want 91 GW of capacity online.

Reality Check

Counting Problem

Wind at 200 MW nameplate might only contribute 30 MW of "firm" capacity at peak. Solar helps during the day but is near zero at 7 PM. ELCC (Effective Load-Carrying Capability) adjusts nameplate to realistic peak contribution.

ERCOT's Adequacy Tools

Without a capacity market, ERCOT relies on a stack of mechanisms:

  • ORDC/scarcity pricing: High prices during shortage signal need for new capacity
  • Performance Credit Mechanism (PCM): Proposed formal capacity payment — generators earn credits for performance during scarcity hours; LSEs must buy credits
  • Texas Energy Fund (TEF): State fund providing low-interest loans/grants to build dispatchable generation (gas plants). Approved 2023, first applications 2025
  • RUC/conservative operations: ERCOT commits extra generators out-of-market as a buffer
  • Demand response: ERS and industrial load programs add effective capacity at the margin
The 400+ GW in ERCOT's interconnection queue as of 2025 looks impressive — but most of it is speculative. Mostly solar, storage, and wind. Very little new dispatchable gas. If load growth from data centers and LNG materializes as projected while new dispatchable capacity doesn't, reserve margins could turn negative by end of decade. This is the argument for PCM and TEF.
Section 11

Grid Emergencies & EEA

When things go wrong, ERCOT has a tiered response system. Understanding these levels is crucial — both for knowing when the grid is in trouble and for understanding why operators make the decisions they do.

Hospital Code system: Emergency alert levels are like hospital codes — Code Blue isn't called until the patient is crashing. EEA Level 1 is the first alarm. EEA Level 3 is rolling blackouts. Between those levels are a series of escalating interventions designed to pull the system back from the edge before reaching the worst outcome.

EEA1

Level 1 — Reserves Below 2,300 MW

ERCOT halts non-essential maintenance, issues public conservation appeals, uses DC tie imports if available. Watch and prepare mode — no customer impact yet.

EEA2

Level 2 — Reserves Below 1,750 MW

Deploy all Non-Spin. Activate ERS (emergency demand response). ERS-30 loads are tripped. Public told rotating outages are possible. Prices spike. Large industrial loads reduce consumption.

EEA3

Level 3 — Load Exceeds Available Generation

ERCOT orders TDSPs to shed firm load. Rolling blackouts begin — distribution utilities open feeders in rotating blocks (~20–30 min on/off) to protect the remainder of the grid from total collapse. Critical facilities (hospitals) on protected feeders.

Winter Storm Uri — February 2021

What Happened

~48% of Generation Capacity Offline

Unprecedented cold caused generator equipment to freeze (gas plants, some wind turbines). Gas supply simultaneously collapsed as wellheads froze and compression stations lost power. Demand hit ~76 GW.

Consequence

~20 GW of Load Shed for Days

Millions without power for 3–4 days in freezing temperatures. Hundreds of deaths. Billions in economic damage. Multiple small REP bankruptcies from $9,000/MWh pricing.

Root Cause 1

Weatherization Failure

Power plants weren't winterized. Recommended after the 2011 cold event — not mandated. Post-Uri, SB3 made weatherization mandatory with inspections and fines up to $1M/day/violation.

Root Cause 2

Gas-Electric Coordination

Gas supply froze → generators lost fuel → load shed cut power to gas compressors → more gas froze. A vicious cycle. Now addressed with critical facility designations and data-sharing protocols.

ERCOT monitored frequency down to ~59.3 Hz during Uri — dangerously close to the ~58.5 Hz threshold where mass under-frequency relays would have tripped, potentially causing an uncontrolled total grid collapse that could have taken weeks to restore. The operators made decisions in real-time to prevent that scenario.
Section 12

Renewables & Storage

Texas leads the US in wind capacity and is racing ahead in solar and battery storage. Integrating this much variable generation creates operational challenges that are reshaping the market.

Wind

~38 GW

Mostly Panhandle and West Texas. Best output at night and in spring/fall — often poorest at summer afternoon peak.

Solar

16–20 GW

Rapid growth. Helps at summer peak (sunny days = AC days). Near zero after sunset. Creates steep evening ramp.

Battery Storage

~5 GW (2023)

Mostly 1–2 hour lithium-ion. Revenues dominated by Reg and RRS (fast response). Growing rapidly — projections of 10+ GW by late 2020s.

Renewable Share

~30% Annual

Wind + solar together in 2022. Growing toward 50%+ by end of decade at current build rates. Record: over 67% instantaneous penetration at times.

The Duck Curve Problem

Solar produces massively during midday, pushing net load down. When solar sets in the late afternoon, net load ramps back up sharply just as demand peaks (dinner time, ACs still running). This creates the "duck curve" — a deep midday trough followed by a steep evening ramp. ECRS was introduced partly to handle this ramp. Gas plants and batteries must respond in 1–3 hours.

Inertia — The Hidden Risk

Synchronous generators (gas, coal, nuclear) have massive spinning rotors that resist frequency changes — inertia. Solar and wind are inverter-based and provide no natural inertia. As more synchronous generators go offline and are replaced by renewables, the grid loses inertia. Result: when a large plant trips, frequency drops faster. ERCOT monitors total system inertia and maintains a minimum threshold of online synchronous generation to preserve grid stability.

Battery Storage Participation

Revenue Source

Ancillary Services First

In early 2023, ~87% of Texas battery revenue came from AS — primarily Regulation (fast response is their superpower) and RRS. Energy arbitrage (charge cheap, sell expensive) is secondary.

Emerging Issue

State of Charge Optimization

Without a multi-interval real-time market, batteries might discharge during a 5-min price spike at 4 PM and be empty when the real peak hits at 6 PM. IMM is pushing for MIRTM (lookahead dispatch) to fix this.

Batteries in ERCOT are classified as Electric Storage Resources (ESR) — they pay LMP when charging, earn LMP when discharging, and don't pay retail transmission charges for charging. This regulatory structure, clarified in 2021, was a major enabler of the Texas storage boom.
Section 13

Risk Management

ERCOT prices can go from $25 to $5,000 in a single day. Every serious participant has a risk management framework. Here's how they think about it.

Insurance and forwards: Risk management in power markets is like combining insurance (protection against rare catastrophes) with forward contracts (locking in prices before you know what happens). You might use a weather derivative to hedge the risk of an unusually hot summer, a power swap to lock in your selling price, and a CRR to hedge your basis risk. Layer enough of these and you transform a volatile, unpredictable cash flow into something more stable.

Power Purchase Agreement (PPA) Long-term price risk — locks a fixed price for 10–15 years Renewable developers (need revenue certainty to finance projects), corporate buyers (ESG commitments, cost stability)
Futures & Swaps (ICE/CME) Hub price risk for months/seasons ahead Generators locking in revenue, retailers capping costs; financial traders providing liquidity
CRRs / PTPs Congestion basis risk between node and hub/zone Wind farms with West Texas basis exposure, retailers in load zones, traders speculating on congestion
Heat Rate Swaps Spark spread (power price minus gas cost) Gas plant operators — locks in margin rather than betting on either commodity independently
Options / Insurance Extreme tail events ($500+ prices, extended scarcity) Retailers with fixed-rate customer contracts, large industrials with exposure to extended outages
4CP Load Reduction Annual transmission charge allocation Large industrials — reducing usage during the 4 highest 15-min peaks each summer can dramatically cut their transmission bill for the next year
After Winter Storm Uri, some retailers had unfixed-rate customers exposed to $9,000/MWh pricing for 4+ days. Several small REPs went bankrupt. The counterparty risk cascaded — ERCOT had to socialize ~$3B in defaults across remaining participants. Post-Uri rules require much stricter collateral and credit management. The exposure limits are real.

Natural Gas Hedging — Don't Ignore It

Texas is ~55% gas generation. Gas prices directly drive power prices. A spike in Henry Hub affects ERCOT clearing prices within days. Fuel hedging (buying gas forwards, securing firm supply contracts, maintaining on-site fuel oil for dual-fuel units) isn't just financial risk management — it's a reliability strategy. Uri demonstrated that unavailable fuel is indistinguishable from an offline plant.

Section 14

Settlement & Billing

Every megawatt-hour has to be accounted for and paid. ERCOT's settlement system turns 5-minute dispatch decisions into final invoices.

Credit card statement vs bank records: The initial settlement (OD+2) is like your real-time bank balance — quick, approximate, based on best available data. The final settlement (OD+55) is like your official statement — audited, corrected, definitive. The true-up resettlement (~OD+180) handles any discovered errors that slipped through both. Each supersedes the last.

What's on a Settlement Statement

Settlement statements break down every charge type. Major components:

  • Energy deviations: For each 15-min interval, your generation credited at nodal LMP; your load charged at zone LMP. DA positions net out first.
  • Ancillary services: AS awards paid at clearing price. Performance factors for Regulation (did you follow AGC signals?). Non-performance penalties.
  • RUC make-whole: If ERCOT committed you via RUC and your market revenues didn't cover costs, you get a make-whole payment. This cost socializes as uplift to all loads.
  • CRR credits/debits: Congestion rents distributed to CRR holders.
  • ORDC adder: The shortage adder charged to loads and credited to online generators in each interval.
  • System administration fee: Small per-MWh fee ERCOT charges to fund its operations.
If you think a meter reading or price is wrong, you generally have 10 days from the final settlement to file a dispute. ERCOT reviews it and can issue a resettlement if an error is confirmed. Missing that window usually means you're stuck with the result.
Section 15

Post-Uri Reforms

The 2021 crisis broke things and forced Texas to rethink. Here's a summary of what changed, what's in progress, and what's still contested.

Done (2021–2022)

Weatherization Mandates

SB3 requires all generators to file winterization plans and pass inspections. PUCT can fine up to $1M/day/violation. By winter 2022–23, nearly full compliance. No significant weather-related outages in subsequent cold snaps.

Done (2022)

ORDC Recalibration

Price cap to $5,000. MCL raised to 3,000 MW. Phase 1 of PUCT's Blueprint. Generated ~$1.7B extra revenue to generators in 2022 vs old curve.

In Progress

Performance Credit Mechanism (PCM)

Approved conceptually. Design details being finalized. Target implementation 2025–2026. Will pay generators for availability during scarcity hours. Critics worry about cost without guaranteed new builds.

In Progress

Texas Energy Fund (TEF)

Up to $10B in state-backed loans/grants for new dispatchable capacity. First applications in 2025. Goal: 2–5 GW of new gas peakers as a backstop fleet.

Under Study

Real-Time Co-Optimization

Joint energy + AS dispatch in real-time. Rejected in 2019 as too expensive. With more batteries and renewables, the IMM says the math has changed. ERCOT is re-evaluating.

Under Study

FFR as Standalone AS Product

Currently procured within RRS. A separate product would allow more battery and load participation in ultra-fast frequency response. Especially relevant as system inertia declines.

The next 3–5 years are when the reforms either prove themselves or get revisited. Data center and industrial load growth is the wildcard. If reserve margins tighten faster than new capacity comes online, expect PUCT to act aggressively — possibly mandating capacity contracts for the first time in ERCOT's history.
